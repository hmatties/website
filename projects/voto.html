Context

Voto is a web app designed to help younger voters enhance their understanding and engagement in local politics.

Voto utilizes a user‚Äôs location to curate political content to their sphere of influence. This content can cover elections, officials, current issues, events, registration, and engagement opportunities. Voto leverages multiple civic api‚Äôs to access this information and create the perfect civic platform to help young voters make an impact in their community.

Problem

Voter turnout in the United States is low.

In large national elcetions we tend to see participation around 60%. That number, however, drastically drops when looking at local elections. Nationally, only 15-27% of eligable voters participate in local elections.

Along with the general lack of participation, this phenomena is only worsened as we assess participation by age demographic. Less than half of all voters between the ages of 18-29 participated in the most recent election, coming in at an average of 19 points below older demographics.

Analysizing Competitors

The competitors considered all focussed on voter participation in some capacity, each featured a unique combination of offerings that sought to either engage or educate the user in some capacity. We also took a look at the age demographics of the users each competitor was targeting to get a better sense of how we could approach building a strong connection with our expected demographic

User Surveys

We utilized surveys to help get a clearer picture of how Gen-z and young millenials interact with politics. Questions covered users participation frequency, motivations, tool affinity, and more. The survey encompassed 15 questions and we received 74 responses.

Research Synthesis

After evaluating our survey results and competitor‚Äôs offerings, we began to identify some key themes in which we could begin categorizing our users core problems. These problems were framed to easily map directly to a core component of our solution. These core problems and associated solutions can be seen below.

Finding Information
Problem

Users found it difficult to find the platform, history, and responsibility of a particular candidate or elected official in a digestable and readily available format.

Representatives
Solution

Provide users an overview of the roles and associated responsibilities of officials and candidates in your area. This would allow users to build a deeper undersanding of who the actors within their system are and how they can influence the process.

Knowledge Gap
Problem

Users may lack knowledge of key issues in their area, how they make affect them, and where they may have an oppurtunity to participate.

Issues
Solution

Provide users an overview of key issues in their area. This content would cover laws, mandates, news, and much more. The focus here is to provide a context for users to feel comfortable talking about issues in their area.

Event Participation
Problem

Users have a hard time knowing and/or remembering when and where elections or events are happening in their area.

Calendar
Solution

Provide users an overview of key upcoming events and opportunities in their area. This content would cover elections, public meetings, volunteer opportunities, and more.

Apathy
Problem

People are generally apathetic about participating. This could be for a multitude of reasons, including a combination of all the ones found above.

Engagement
Solution

Provide users an avenue for all things voting, registration, and volunteering. By conglomerating this content in an seamless way, we can help fix components of participation apathy.

The design process valued clarity over anything else. Not only was the topic itself extremely dense, but the content was also subject to misrepresentation as we have seen through other media outlets. This phase of the product utilized every tool possible, including information architecture, building user flows, rounds of wireframing, and information architecture testing.

Defining Hierarchy

After evaluating our survey results and competitor‚Äôs offerings, we began to identify some key themes in which we could begin categorizing our users core problems. These problems were framed to easily map directly to a core component of our solution. These core problems and associated solutions can be seen below.

Managing Shared Content

After defining a hierarchy across the entire application, it was clear that there was a large amount of content that would be shared or referenced in multiples areas of the site. To clarify where and how this content would be shared, user flows were utilized to refine its usage.

Wireframing

Once I had built a concise information hierarchy, and thought through management of key content throughout the site, I was then able to begin building wireframes. This was an iterative process, going through three ideation phases before moving to visual design. All component and page concepts were built in Whimsical.

Testing

After developing multiple rounds of wireframes, refining feature offerings, and restructuring content hierarchy we were able to analyze some key areas that could potentially cause confusion to some users. To remove these hurdles before visual design, we utilized tree jack tests to refine the information hierarchy.

Component Building

Along with UX strategy, I also provided support on the design system for Voto. Here we built standardized component systems that could be used throughout the site. These components were also built to be interactive, which allowed our click-through prototype to be more immersive and better embody a fully built app.

Takeaways

Utility builds form

Especially when working with this type of information, it‚Äôs important to remember that content is king. I found through this process that often the hierarchy and relationships between content shows a pattern that helps you clearly understand how content should be displayed.

Communication is key

Multidisciplinary projects often require a lot of back and forth, but I found especially in this hybrid work model we see today, it is more important than ever to utilize all the communication tools available to you. Not only are video call tools great for this, but asynchronous tools like Slack and Figma allow team members to communicate without face time.

Design for data

Data is essential for any modern web tool. This is something I was aware of before this project, but my understanding of its relationship to design shifted in this project. A key thing I realized is that this implementation and integration of data is essential to consider in the early steps of a project, as the usage and sourcing of it can, and should, alter the representation of content within the product.
