Context

Boa is an entryway coat rack bench for the modern home.

Boa utilizes a plush nest to bring a beautiful and novel storage system into the home. This special topics studio was led by world renowned furniture designer, Sean Yoo. His studios focus on developing beautiful and functional furniture from deep and meaningful inspiration. Each project is design and built in the span of a single semester.

Prompt

Design a piece of furniture inspired by a film, song, or place.

This unique methodology has been utilized by Sean Yoo throughout his entire career. It helps designers pull inspiration from their own experiences, and in turn, creates a piece true to their individuality and design philosophy.

Ideation

Designing From Experience

Designing anything requires a starting point, a seed of inspiration that allows you a platform to build upon. Here we looked at some of the most meaningful and inspiring things one might carry with them; our favorite places, songs, and films. Our ideation was structued. We were asked to develop ten core ideas, or concepts, from each source which would then be evaluated, expanded upon, and assessed for their value in the project.

My Inspiration

With having identified key sources to inspire my piece, we quickly ideated around each. Through weeks of concepting we being the narrow our focus of concepts and sources. Through this ideation process I chose to focus on a piece of music, specifically: First Love / Late Spring by Mitski.

This song reminded me of so much, but specifically, a certain person, and a past love. The memories with this song and artist were incredibly vivid, and the attatchment to this person further fueled the conceptual focus of this phase.

Finally having found a focus and inspiration, the visual design and ideation began. This process utilized countless sketches, models, renderings, and prototypes to finally come to a place where full production could take place.

Sketching

Discerning form always begins with sketching. Here, it is no different. Boa's initial form was bred from this 'nest' motif seen in many of the visuals below. This not only acted as a novel storge method, but beautifully captured the suffocating and entangling nature of love that Mistki speaks to, and I experienced.

Thinking 3-Dimensionally

If a picture is worth 1000 words, a prototype is worth 1000 pictures. The use of scaled models allowed me to think of form from all angles, and identify certain angles not previously considered.

Human Factors

Though this project focusses on developing a beautifully crafted object, one cant neglect the untility of a piece of furniture. It has to serve the user, no matter how beautiful it may be.

Here I set out to test as many node variations as I could. I experimented with countless variations and combinations of fabrics and foams to land on a final materiality that provides adequate structure while remaining maleable. Building a full-scale mockups and funcitonality prototypes allowed me to understand key dimensions and where I may have neglected certain interaction points.

Form Refinement

Through multiple rounds of critque and prototpying, Boa's final form was chosen. These visualizations highlight some of the key details incorporated in the final prototype.

The key details highlighted here are, first, the modularity of the peice. This two part build is intended to make easier to handle, and feasible to build. The second key detail was the addition of the lip on the shoe carriage. This lip provides extra support for the tower, and provides a unqiue visual 'echo' between the bench and tower.

Build

Production

Boa's final prototype production began by creating the initial forms for both the bench and the coat rack tower.

This initial phase of production required a lot of on-the-spot problem solving. Jig making, form refinement, and material shifts were all essential in the process. Boa's base shape is made from maple plywood and 1/4 rounds, seamed together with oak splines.

Finish

Once the base form of both the tower and the bench were fully built, I then began work on the final finish surface and key funcitonality.

The intended finish was a beautiful white maple veneer. Becuase of the materials delicacy, I had to assure the surface was free from imperfections and uniform. This was done using body filler. With the veneer and edge banding finished, the last step was to sew and stuff the plush nodes used in the coat rack.

Takeaways

Leave a fingerprint

Every person carries with them their own experiences that provide unique insights and perspectives on the world around them, and designers are no exception. By seeking anonymity in your work, you rid design of its humanity. Good design is not arbitrary, it encompasses a thorough utilization of inspiration, research, iteration, and craft. Regardless of medium, each step in the this process provides us designers an opportunity to highlight our individuality and leave our fingerprint.

Compartmentalize exploration

Many projects, like boa, present a multitude of challenges to accomplish a single goal. Within projects like these, I learned that organization and compartmentalization are key to allow each piece of the puzzle fit together well. Throughout this project I sought to find isolated components that could be explored in smaller environments. This allowed me to not only save time, but also allowed me to for-see facets of the project that pose more of a challenge than others. By approaching challenges in this way, I was able to create a more polished final project and simultaneously provide myself the ability to speak to the combination and interaction of these features in a more alluring way.

Eliminate expectations

Long term projects like this one often bring with them a long list of expectations regarding output, timeline, and challenges at their inception. Designing and building Boa taught me to forgo all of these preconceived conclusions. These challenges throughout the project altered my timeline, my craft, and even the form of Boa. These challenges not only helped me grow as a designer, but allowed the project itself to visually evolve in ways I never thought possible. Boa not only taught me to account for these challenges, but to embrace them.
