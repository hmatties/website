Context

Gami is a flat-packing parametric furniture series. The origami-inspired series can be built into footstools, barstools, coffee tables, night stands, and more.

Gami‚Äôs simple, yet elegant form fits perfectly into any modern home, and can especially help those looking to save space. Gami uses computation to build its form and optimize its production, specifically, a parametric methodolgy is utilized here.

The core of Gami‚Äôs research lied in two distinct places. One, being the fundamentals of computational design, specifically, parametricism. The other core research category lied in understanding traditional origami practices, this required a lot of hand and scaled modeling to understand how rigid structure could be created from 2D patterns.

Utilizing a Parametric Methodology

Traditional computational methods, like generative design, rely on code to drive the form of an object. Gami utilizes a parametric ideology which allows designers to make overarching changes to their design in real-time.

This parametric methodology allowed me to maintain my control over the form of Gami, while also being able to easily manipulate the scale and fit of whatever form factor the user might desire. This computational usage represents one component of Gami‚Äôs creation. Parametric design was primarily used here to facilitate the production of the many offshoots and form factors Gami can take. The other primary ideation method revolved around origami inspired direct modeling to discern the form that Gami would take.

Ideation

Ideating Through Making

The other major component to Gami‚Äôs creation revolved around direct modeling. Though computation was used heavily here, I still sought to utilize an iterative process of making to identify a unique and beautiful final form for Gami.

A majority of my research and inspiration came from studying traditional origami patterns and forms. Upon coming to a consensus on the final form of Gami, I then implemented the concept in code, which allowed me to seamlessly adjust parameters as necessary.

Scripting

Scripting

Building Gami‚Äôs script required a basic understanding of object-oriented programming, and a deep understanding of the underlying geometry and trigonometry that builds the form of Gami. Utilizing mathematical syntax was essential to allow Gami to truly act parametrically.

Scripting overview

Gami was built using Grasshopper, a visual, node-base, scripting plugin for rhino 3D. Below you can see both a visual representation of how the form is parametrically being built, and the raw nodes building gami.

Grasshopper

Gami‚Äôs node structure builds form from point relationships. All these relationships are based on the initial bounding box, which is defined by the user, or preset standardized sizes. From there, all the panels and lid are then built to fit and fold perfectly. At certain checkpoints throughout the script the designer can intervene to account for various manufacturing constraints.

Gami

Flat-packing Functionality

Gami is designed to be packed flat for storage and travel. When being built, it utilizes a convenient zipper to form the body and a steel tension hook to give the form rigidity.

No matter the scale or form factor, Gami utilizes the same tools to create its form. The build process is quick and easy, with the core steps outlined below. Simply zip up the body, place on the lid, and pull the tension clip.

Takeaways

Personalization is the future of product

The adoption of computation in the design process allows us creators to work with data in ways before unseen. This not only allows us to optimize the ideation and production process, but also allows us to provide users with unique and personalized products. This focus in nature has the product adapt to the individual user, rather than the user adapting to the product and its constraints.

Designing with data is essential

Data is everywhere. Though not a replacement for traditional qualitative design research methods, it is an extremely useful tool that can enhance outcomes of any design process.

Computation/novel technology aids the designer, not directs them

Novel technologies, like computational design tools, can often be deceptive in how they can and should be used. Their true strength lies in their support of the designer, not their control. forgoing this ideal results in a world that suffocates the fingerprint, or the identity of a designer.
